ROLEPLAYING IS A CONVERSATION. The GM sets the scene, you describe what you do, she tells you how the NPCs react, you respond, and so on – back and forth. The story grows one step at a time, and everyone lives happily ever after.

Yeah, just kidding about that last part. Not on the Frontier, you don’t.

Sooner or later, the shit – just like prisoner Murphy – hits the fan. Things come to a head, a point of no return is reached, and a problem mounts that cannot just be settled by talking your way out of it.

That’s when you call upon your mad skills. Just break out the dice and show Mother you know a thing or two.

USING SKILLS

THERE ARE TWELVE SKILLS in total in the game. Three skills are typically linked to each of the four attributes: Strength, Agility, Wits, and Empathy. Skills are measured in skill levels, from 0 to 5. You can increase your skill levels during play (page 30).

Dice

Two types of six-sided dice are used in this game: base dice and stress dice. You can use any normal six-sided dice if you don’t have access to the custom dice for the game, but it helps if you have them in two different colors to tell base dice and stress dice apart.

When To Roll

Generally, the rules in this book or the adventure text will indicate when to roll dice and what skill to roll for. However, the GM can always call for a roll in a situation she feels warrants it. We recommend against excessive dice rolling however, saving the rolls for dramatic and tense situations where something is truly at stake.

Means Success

To use a skill, add your skill level to your score in the attribute linked to the skill, and grab that many base dice. Then roll all the dice together. For your action to succeed, you must roll at least one 6 ( on the custom dice) – if not, your action fails.

Stress Dice:

If you have stress level 1 or higher, add this number of stress dice to the dice pool as well. If you roll a 1 on one or more stress dice ( on the custom dice), you suffer a stress response – see page 44.

NPCS & Skills

Non-player characters use skills in the same way as player characters, with the one major difference being that NPCs never push rolls. This means NPCs don’t have a stress level.

The GM rolls dice for NPCs, but should only roll for actions that affect a PC directly – for example, if the NPC is attacking a player character or is attempting to save them. When an NPC performs an action that does not directly affect a PC, the GM can simply decide what happens, without rolling dice.

Switching Attributes

If the GM or adventure text calls for it, you may need to use a different attribute than the one normally linked to a skill. For example, threatening an NPC may use Strength + MANIPULATION instead of Empathy + MANIPULATION. You can even suggest rolling for a different attribute yourself if the situation calls for it. The GM has final say. Note that some talents specifically allow a different attribute to be used with a certain skill.

Rolls Without Skill:

If you don’t have the skill required for the particular action you want to perform, you can roll anyway – simply roll a number of base dice equal to your attribute as well as any stress dice.

Modifiers

The rules or the GM can modify your chances, adding or removing base dice for you to roll. This is referred to as +1 die, +2 dice, etc., and conversely –1 die, –2 dice, etc. You can never go below one base die. Modifiers never affect stress dice.

Gear:

Equipment can modify your roll, but not always. Sometimes a specific item is needed just to perform the action. Weapons and other types of gear are found in chapter 5.

Helping

Up to three other PCs and NPCs can help you with a dice roll. Each person helping you gives you +1 die, but they need to be in a position to actually support your action in a concrete way. The GM has final say. In combat, helping someone uses up your full action for the round.

Example
Skill Modifiers and Helping

The air scrubbers on the USCSS Miranda are offline and our roughneck Nat tries to repair them. With Strength 5 and skill level 3 in HEAVY MACHINERY, she gets eight base Dices. She uses a maintenance jack for the job and Jeffries helps her out, giving her +2 Dice for a whopping total of ten base dice.

Multiple Successes

The more you roll, the better the result. In combat (page 59), that usually means more damage, but it can mean any number of bonus effects – for example:

  • You show off.
  • You do it quickly.
  • You do it quietly.
  • You achieve some unexpected additional effect.

The adventure text or the GM has final say on what extra can be used for.

Failure

If you don’t roll any , you mess up. For some reason, you failed to achieve your goal. Feel free to elaborate on why with the help of the GM. She might even decide that a failed roll has further consequences that will move the story forward in a dramatic way.

Failure should never stop the story completely. Even when you fail, there must be a way forward – perhaps at the cost of time, risk, or W-Y dollars, but still a way. The GM has the final say on the consequences of failure in any particular situation.

You have one last chance if you really want to succeed – you can push the roll.

Pushing Your Roll

If you fail a roll, or need more , you can choose to push the roll. This immediately increases your stress level (page 72) one step, meaning you add one stress die to your dice pool. Then re-roll all dice not showing . All dice count after the re-roll, including the ones not re-rolled.

Stress Dice:

If you rolled one or more on stress dice in the initial roll, you cannot push the roll.

Just Once:

You can only push your roll once, unless you have a talent that allows you to push a roll a second time.

Stress Response

If you roll on one or more stress dice in a skill roll, a stress response is triggered. Roll a D6, adding your stress level and subtracting your Resolve score (page 26) before comparing the result to the table below. Your stress responses are removed by relieving stress (page 73). If you get a stress response that you already have, you instead gain stress level +1.

Passive Rolls

Sometimes, the GM will ask you to roll for something you’re not even aware of – typically OBSERVATION rolls in stealth mode (page 56). Such rolls cannot be pushed, nor trigger stress responses. You still add your stress level to the roll. The GM can even make some passive rolls in secret.

Opposed Rolls

When facing an active resistance to your skill roll, you need to roll off against your opponent. In an opposed roll, you need to roll more than your opponent to succeed. You can push the roll even if you’re the target of an opposed roll. Opposed rolls are used sparingly, only when indicated by the rules or the adventure text, or when deemed appropriate by the GM.

PvP:

In the case of two PCs rolling off against each other, the active PC decides whether to push the roll first, and if so, re-rolls, then the passive PC decides whether to push or not.

Open Rolls:

In opposed rolls where there is no clear active party, a tie means there is no clear winner of the roll. If this doesn’t work in the situation, break the tie randomly.

Example
Opposed Rolls

Captain Charlize on the USCSS Miranda is attempting to bypass Mother's antagonistic actions by accessing her core in the mainframe. The GM says this is an opposed roll against Mother's COMTECH skill level of 7 (the ship's MU/TH/UR 7000 mainframe has no base attributes). Charlize has no COMTECH skill, so she's just Rolling four base Dice for her Wits and four stress Dice for the current stress level. Her roll comes up with one , but the GM rolls for Mother and get two .

Charlize decides to push the roll, increasing her stress level to 5 and adding Another stress die to her dice pool. She then re-rolls all dice not showing , getting two more for a total of three! However, she also rolls one on a stress die. With a Resolve score of 4 and stress level 5, she rolls a D6 and adds 1. In a stroke of bad luck, she rolls a 6 for a total of 7. The captain messes up! On the brink of triumph, Mother takes control of the USCSS Miranda and her Communications. As a result, Charlize gains +1 stress level.

Story Points

In a cinematic adventure, you can spend a story point (page 158) to automatically get a result on one base die in a skill roll. This can be done before or after pushing the roll. You gain story points by following your personal agenda (page 27). You can never have more than 3 story points.

THE SKILLS

Heavy Machinery (Strength)

Hard work is part of life for any roughneck on the Frontier, but at least you have powerful machines to help you. Roll for this skill when you attempt to use, repair, jury-rig or break any kind of heavy machinery.

Stamina (Strength)

When your physical endurance or vigor is tested, roll for STAMINA. For example, this skill is used to survive the cold vacuum of space or to resist a deadly contagion.

Close Combat (Strength)

The world of ALIEN is a brutal place. Sometimes, you have no choice but to fight for your life, up close and personal. Roll for this skill when you attack someone in close combat. Read more about close combat in chapter 4.

Mobility (Agility)

Roll for MOBILITY to perform any kind of acrobatic movement such as a risky climb or a dangerous jump.

Piloting (Agility)

Be it a dropship, a star freighter or a battle frigate, you’re the one to fly it. Roll for PILOTING when you attempt any difficult and dangerous maneuver at the helm of any type of spacecraft (page 143). The skill can also be used to drive ground vehicles.

Ranged Combat (Agility)

If you have a ranged weapon, you can take down your enemy from a distance without getting your hands bloody. Use the RANGED COMBAT skill to fire all types of ranged weapons. Read more about ranged combat in chapter 4.

Comtech (Wits)

Programming androids, mainframes, and other types of advanced technology requires specialist knowledge. Roll for COMTECH for any challenging attempt to program, repair, decrypt or otherwise manipulate any type of computer or communications technology.

Observation (Wits)

In the world of ALIEN, you need to be on your guard at all times, or you won’t live long. Use your OBSERVATION skill to spot someone sneaking. You can also use the skill when you spot an unknown threat of some kind, to learn more about it. Read more about stealth mode in the next chapter.

Survival (Wits)

Terraforming can make the atmosphere on alien worlds (passably) breathable, but it will rarely make them Earthlike. You might be able to breathe without a pressure suit on such worlds, though you can also expect to struggle against extreme heat and cold, sandstorms, acid rain, and other extreme weather. Roll for SURVIVAL when you’re in a hazardous environment to avoid becoming fatigued – or worse (page 75).

Command (Empathy)

If you are to survive the horrors of space, you’ll need a good leader – or you must become one yourself. You can use your COMMAND skill to stop panic in other PCs (page 72) and to give orders in combat (page 60).

Manipulation (Empathy)

Alien life forms may gut you without remorse, but the most truly dangerous beings in the ALIEN universe are corporate agents and other schemers who use lies, threats, or subtle persuasion to get what they want. To make another person see things your way, roll for MANIPULATION. If the person is actively opposed to your point of view, this is an opposed MANIPULATION roll (page 44). Your chances are affected by your negotiating position (see the adjacent sidebar).

Not Mind Control

When you use MANIPULATION on someone, you don’t take control of their mind. What you are trying to convince your adversary to do must be somewhat reasonable, or the GM can disallow it.

Medical Aid (Empathy)

In the world of ALIEN, there is a significant risk that you or the other characters will be injured, sooner or later. Roll for MEDICAL AID to give first aid to a dying character (page 68) or to make medical examinations.

negotiating Position

Your chances of using MANIPULATION on someone successfully are affected by your negotiating position.

Each of the following factors gives you +1 die:

  • You have more people on your side.
  • What you ask for doesn’t cost your opponent anything.
  • Your opponent has suffered damage.
  • You have helped your opponent previously.
  • You present your case very well (determined by the GM).

Each of the following factors gives you −1 die:

  • Your opponent has more people on their side.
  • You ask for something valuable or dangerous.
  • Your opponent has nothing to gain by helping you.
  • You are having trouble understanding each other.
  • You are talking via radio or shouting at a distance.

TALENTS

TO SURVIVE AND THRIVE in the cold dark of space you need to find your own niche, something you and no one else can do. Such special abilities are called talents.

Talents can affect how you use skills or how you recover from damage or stress, or even let you do things that are quite simply impossible for others. Unlike skills, talents are not rated – you either have them or you don’t.

There are two distinct types of talents – career talents and general talents. Career talents can only be learned by characters with a specific career. General talents can be learned by anyone. You can learn new talents during play (page 30).

Colonial Marine Talents

  • Banter:

    The stress level of you and everyone in Short range drops two steps (instead of one) for every stretch of time spent in a safe place (page 73).

  • Overkill:

    When you roll 9 or higher on a panic roll (page 72), you can ignore the result and instead go into overkill. All other PCs in the same zone must make an immediate panic roll. While in overkill, you must attack your enemies on every turn and you cannot take cover, dodge, or defend yourself against attacks. The effect stops when you or all enemies in sight are broken.

  • Precise Shooter:

    When making a supply roll for ammo (page 66), you get to roll two dice fewer than your ammo supply rating, to a minimum of one die.

Colonial Marshal Talents

  • Authority:

    You can use your COMMAND skill instead of MANIPULATION to get someone to bend to your will. You also get +2 dice to the roll.

  • Investigator:

    You get +2 die to OBSERVATION rolls when actively searching for something hidden in a zone (page 57). This does not apply to passive OBSERVATION rolls.

  • Subdue:

    When attempting to grapple an opponent in close combat (page 62), you get +2 dice.

Company Agent Talents

  • Company Resources:

    Once per act in cinematic play or once per session in campaign play, you may gain +1 stress level to produce an item of your choice – you had it all along! It can be any item from chapter 5, or anything else the GM deems reasonable, but it cannot be larger than a regular item (weight 1). The item is not yours – the Company will want it back.

  • Cunning:

    You can roll for MANIPULATION using Wits instead of Empathy.

  • Personal Safety:

    If you are attacked and another PC or friendly NPC is within Short range, make a MANIPULATION roll (no action) to make the other character be targeted by the attack instead of you. Stress level +1 for each use.

Kid Talents

  • Hard to Catch:

    You get +2 dice to MOBILITY when dodging an attack (page 65).

  • Lucky:

    You get to re-roll any death roll or roll for a critical injury once (page 70). Choose the result you prefer.

  • Nimble:

    You can push a skill roll using Agility twice. Stress level +1 for each push.

  • Nurse:

  • Surgeon:

  • Field Commander:

  • Pull Rank:

Pilot Talents

  • Like The Back Of Your Hand:

  • Reckless:

  • Steady Hands:

Scientist Talents

  • Analysis:

    You can roll for OBSERVATION to gain insight into alien artifacts or creatures that you examine for at least one stretch. For every you roll, you may ask the GM one of the following questions: Is it human or alien? Is it dead or alive? How old is it? What is its purpose? How does it work? What problems could it cause? The GM must answer truthfully, but is allowed to give vague or incomplete answers. A successful roll reduces the stress level of all other PCs within Short range 1 step, while a failed roll increases their stress levels by 1. Only one attempt is allowed per examined object.

  • Inquisitive:

    You can push a skill roll based on Wits twice. Stress level +1 for each push.

  • Xenomorphology:

    When facing an extraterrestrial creature of any kind, you may make an OBSERVATION roll (quick action). If successful, you realize a potential weakness of the creature, if it has any. If you fail, gain stress level +1. Only one attempt per Xenomorph is allowed.

General Talents

  • Bodyguard:

    If someone in the same zone as you is hit by an attack, you can spend a quick action to dive in to take the hit instead.

  • Bonus Shares:

    Gain +2 dice to MANIPULATION rolls when negotiating the payment for a job in campaign play (page 244).

  • Calm Breather:

    When making a supply roll for air, you get to roll two dice fewer than your air supply rating (page 28), to a minimum of one die.

  • Combat Veteran:

    In combat, you may keep your initiative card from the previous round instead of drawing a new one.

  • Counselor:

    Once per stretch, you can use the MANIPULATION skill to reduce the stress level of another PC in the same zone. For each rolled, their stress level is reduced by one extra step (in addition to the default one step, see page 73). You cannot use this talent on yourself, nor recover stress in the same stretch as you use it.

  • EVA Specialist:

    You get +2 to HEAVY MACHINERY and MOBILITY rolls when spacewalking.

  • Fast Reflexes:

    When drawing initiative (page 59), draw two cards instead of one. Choose the one you want to use and shuffle the other back into the deck. If several characters have this talent, carry out this process one at a time in any order you prefer. PCs draw before NPCs.

  • Gunner:

    You get a +2 dice to RANGED COMBAT rolls when firing vehicle-mounted weapons or spaceship armaments.

  • Hardened:

    Your maximum Health score is increased by 1. You can choose this talent up to three times.

  • Haymaker:

    You get +2 dice to a CLOSE COMBAT attack if you sacrifice your quick action for the round .

  • Martial Arts:

    When you attack or defend unarmed in CLOSE COMBAT, you can use Agility instead of Strength. You can also make an unarmed attack as a quick action instead of a full action, but with −2 dice.

  • Last One Standing:

    You can rally yourself when broken (page 68) by rolling for STAMINA yourself. Only one attempt can be made.

  • Machinegunner:

    When firing a burst of fully automatic fire (page 67), your stress level does not increase.

  • Menacing:

    When threatening someone to do what you want, roll Strength + MANIPULATION (instead of Empathy) and gain +2 dice.

  • Navigator:

    Roll for COMTECH when plotting a new course for space travel (page 108). For each rolled, the duration of the trip is reduced by 10% (round off to the nearest whole number of days).

  • Pack Mule:

    You can carry twice as many items as normal without being encumbered.

  • Quick Shot:

    If an enemy makes a ranged attack against you, you may as a quick action (reaction) try to shoot them first. Take +1 stress level, then roll for both attacks simultaneously. The shot with the most goes off first. If both shots hit, the second shot is aborted if the shooter is broken. If both shots roll the same amount of , both hit at the same time.

  • Rapid Fire:

    You can fire a pistol (chapter 5) as a quick action instead of a full action, but with −2 dice.

  • Second Wind:

    Anyone who tries to rally you (page 68) gets +2 dice to their COMMAND roll.

  • Seen It All:

    Your Resolve score (page 26) is increased by 1. You can choose this talent up to three times.

  • Sensor Operator:

    Gain +2 dice when rolling for COMTECH to operate ship sensors (page 143).

  • Spaceship Commander:

    In the role as a captain of a spaceship during space combat (page 143), you get +2 dice to COMMAND rolls for initiative and giving orders.

  • Spaceship Mechanic:

    Gain +2 dice when you use HEAVY MACHINERY or COMTECH to maintain or repair damage to a spacecraft (page 144).

  • Stealthy:

    Others get –2 dice to OBSERVATION rolls when trying to spot you in stealth mode (page 56). In groups, the talent has no effect unless everyone in the group has the talent.

  • Stoic:

    You can roll for STAMINA using Wits instead of Strength.

  • Survivor:

    You get +2 dice to all death rolls (page 68).

  • Tech Savvy:

    When making a supply roll for electric power, you get to roll two dice fewer than your power supply rating (page 29), to a minimum of one die.

  • Weapon Specialist:

    You’re an expert at using a specific weapon model – choose one from the weapon lists in chapter 5. When you use this weapon, you get +2 dice. You can choose this talent several times, once per weapon model. You can be a specialist at fighting unarmed.

  • Zero-G Training:

    Your sense of balance is well adapted to an environment without gravity. You don’t suffer any of the penalties to skill rolls in zero-G (page 76).
