FIGHT OR FLIGHT? That’s the big question, now, isn’t it? You may feel invincible tucked away behind your cozy character sheet, but your PC is out there on the line – and they are damn well mortal. Remember back in chapter 2 when we told you not to get your character killed? Consider this your second warning.

Often, it’s a better bet to run away and fight another day – assuming you’re fast enough to escape whatever thing is nipping at your heels. Often, it’s best to just meld into the shadows and be all quiet-like. Before releasing the safety on your pulse rifle, always ask yourself – is it worth it?

Sometimes you have no choice. Sometimes that alien thing you thought was right behind you is already waiting for you in that dark corner you thought was safe.

Just coming face-to-face with an alien monstrosity can turn your mind to jelly. You may only have a split second before the beast dismembers you and crushes your skull. And alien species aren’t the only danger.

People on the Frontier are jerks. Sometimes, someone just sticks a gun in your face. When backed against a wall, you damn well better fight. When someone or something comes for you, you need to be able to defend yourself.

Here’s how.

SPACE & TIME

"Should be in and out in thirty minutes."
Tyler

Maps

A stealth scene or a fight in the ALIEN roleplaying game is typically played out using a map of the starship, colony, or whatever location your characters happen to find themselves fighting for their lives in.

Large, full-color maps of adventure locations are included in the Hope’s Last Day Starter Set and cinematic adventures such as Rapture Protocol, but maps with less detail work as well, even quickly hand-drawn sketches by the GM are fine for more improvised play.

Using Miniatures

For an even more visceral and tangible game experience, official miniatures for the ALIEN roleplaying game are available separately. Together with the large, full-color maps included in the Hope’s Last Day Starter Set and the Rapture Protocol cinematic adventure, the miniatures can even be used to play skirmish-style wargames. Rules for that are available in the miniatures set.

Tokens & Miniatures:

To track movement on the map during stealth or combat, you can use the cardboard tokens in official ALIEN RPG releases like the Hope’s Last Day Starter Set, miniatures such as those included in the ALIEN RPG Miniatures Set, digital tokens on your virtual tabletop, or anything else that works for your table.

Zones

The map is divided into zones. A zone is typically a room, a corridor, or an area of ground. How big a zone is varies – from a few steps across up to about 25 meters. A zone is generally smaller in a cramped environment than in open terrain. In official adventures, zone borders are indicated on the location maps.

Cluttered/ Open:

A zone is either cluttered, meaning there are objects in the zone to hide or take cover in or behind, or open, meaning there are no such objects in the zone. Open zones are typically corridors and crawlspaces, but can also be empty rooms or other open spaces. If the adventure text does not indicate whether a zone is open or cluttered, the GM makes a judgment call.

Borders:

A border between two adjacent zones is either open or blocked (by a wall or bulkhead). A blocked border can have a door or a hatch, as indicated by the map, allowing movement between the two zones. Corridors and crawlspaces are divided into several zones by doors or lines on the map.

Line Of Sight:

Except for characters who are hiding or in full cover, everyone in the same zone has line of sight to each other. This does not apply to narrow crawlspaces like air shafts – there, other characters block line of sight (even friendlies). Open zone borders don’t block line of sight. A blocked border generally blocks line of sight even if there is a door or hatch in it – unless you’re actively standing by the doorway and peeking through.

Playing Without Maps

Although maps can be useful, you can choose not to use them and let certain conflicts play out only in the “theater of the mind.” In this case, the GM needs to determine lines of sight and the distances between combatants.

Measuring Time

In the ALIEN universe, time matters. Whether you are awaiting rescue on an alien world with Xenomorphs stalking you, or Mother is counting down to the imminent destruction of your ship, you need to keep track of time.

Three separate units of time are typically used in the game, depending on the situation at hand. See the table below. The exact duration of a round, stretch and shift can vary depending on the situation.

Countdowns

During a countdown to some catastrophic event, time can be treated as a consumable (page 28). The GM or the adventure text sets the timer to a certain number of minutes. After each combat round, a supply roll is made. For each rolled, the number of minutes remaining is reduced by one.

Stealth Mode

"If you don't stay quiet, we all die."
Andy

IN THE ALIEN ROLEPLAYING GAME, much of the thrill happens before the enemy shows itself and bullets start flying. A key part of the experience is exploring unknown locations, with enemies lurking in the darkness. In the game, this is represented by what we call stealth mode. Stealth mode is played out in stretches of time, each approximately 5–10 minutes long.

PC Movement

Movement in stealth mode is slow and careful. In one stretch of time, your character can move into an adjacent zone on the map (assuming the border is open or has an open door or hatch), scan for enemies, and get a superficial description of the zone from the GM. If you’re a team, you can move individually or as a group. The order in which PCs move is chosen freely.

Searching A Zone:

If you want to examine something in a zone more closely, to search a room or access a data terminal, you may need to stay several stretches in the zone (or even a full shift), if the adventure text or the GM calls for it.

NPC Movement

In stealth mode, NPC movement is managed secretly by the GM each stretch, after your PCs have moved. Human NPCs follow the same rules for movement as PCs, while Xenomorphs may move one zone per point of Speed (page 208) each stretch. The order of NPC movement is chosen by the GM.

Active & Passive:

An adventure text may tell the GM that enemies are active or passive. Active enemies are moving across the map, stalking you or trying to hide from you. Passive enemies are not aware of you and are most often stationary, until they spot you or are otherwise alerted to your presence. Enemies may switch from passive to active, or vice versa, as dictated by the adventure or the GM.

Hidden Map:

To manage NPC movement, we recommend that the GM has a second copy of the map, hidden from view by the GM Screen. On this map, the GM can place tokens to represent NPCs, hidden from the players’ view. GM maps for this purpose are included in official adventures. If the GM doesn’t have access to a screen, they can track hidden NPC movement on a piece of paper

Simplified Stealth Mode

Managing NPC movement secretly by the GM in stealth mode is a great way to create real tension at the table, a true game of cat and mouse – or rather, a game of human and terrifying Xenomorph! However, it does require some table space (or virtual tabletop support) and might not always be feasible. In such cases, the system can be simplified to only have the PCs move according to the stealth mode rules, while NPCs (and motion tracker pings) simply appear when and where the GM so chooses.

Crawlspaces

Air shafts and other crawlspaces are a common feature in ALIEN adventures and present a unique challenge in stealth mode. Crawlspaces can run by the side of rooms and corridors, above them or underneath them (in which case just the outline is shown on official adventure maps).

Access Points:

A crawlspace can be entered by one zone’s worth of movement at any access point marked on the map, just like entering any other zone.

Sealing Access Points:

An access point can typically be sealed shut with a cutting torch by making a HEAVY MACHINERY roll (full action, one attempt per stretch is allowed). Not even Xenomorphs can break through a sealed access point, but acid blood will burn through a crawlspace floor in one stretch (if there is gravity), creating a new access point if the duct is above a room.

Open Zones:

Zones in crawlspaces are generally considered open, meaning it’s not possible to hide there (or take cover in combat, see page 66).

Detection

When you come within line of sight of an NPC in stealth mode, or vice versa, make a passive open opposed OBSERVATION roll. This means the roll cannot be pushed, nor trigger stress responses (page 44). The GM will just ask you to roll OBSERVATION without telling you why, and roll for the NPC.

If one side wins the roll, they spot the other first and can choose to declare their presence, ambush their opponent (page 59), hide in the zone (next page), or back out of the zone the same way they entered. Backing out of the zone in this situation is done immediately and does not count toward the normal movement in this stretch.

On a tied roll, you spot each other at the same time – draw initiative (page 59).

Keeping Guard:

If a person remains stationary in a zone for a stretch and does nothing except keeping guard, they gain +2 dice to OBSERVATION rolls to spot anything coming within line of sight.

Groups In Stealth Mode

When several characters moving as a group come into line of sight of another character (or group), all characters in each group get to roll OBSERVATION. Only the best roll on each side counts. For a group of NPCs with identical stats, the GM will typically make just a single OBSERVATION roll and apply the result to the whole group, to keep things simple.

Hiding

A character can hide in a cluttered zone (but not in an open zone). To hide carefully takes one stretch, and thus cannot be combined with movement or searching the zone – except immediately after a (won) OBSERVATION roll when coming within line of sight of an enemy (see Detection, above). In this specific case only, hiding is “free.” Items can also be hidden in a zone.

Finding What’s Hidden:

When an enemy enters a zone with a hidden character, no opposed OBSERVATION roll is made. Instead, the enemy must actively search the zone for a stretch and make an OBSERVATION roll. If the roll fails, another roll can be made each stretch. Typically, an NPC will only search a zone for one stretch before moving on. If a hidden character is found, draw initiative.

Multiple Searchers:

If several characters search a zone at the same time, they can make separate rolls or let one character roll, and the others help (page 43).

Multiple Hidden Things:

If there are several hidden characters and/or items in a zone, each rolled on the OBSERVATION roll(s) will reveal one of them. The order in which they are found is up to the GM.

Taking Action:

A hidden character may, at their turn in the stretch, take action to move out of the zone, interact with something in the zone, or ambush an enemy. In all such cases, make an opposed OBSERVATION roll against all enemies in line of sight.

Darkness

In dark zones with very dim lightning, OBSERVATION rolls for humans (and androids) are made with –2 dice. Xenomorphs are not affected. A flashlight will remove the effects of darkness in a zone.

Motion Trackers

A motion tracker, such as the M314 unit (page 96), can be used once per stretch. This does not exclude other activities during the stretch, but before the tracker gives any information a power supply roll must be made (page 29).

If still powered up, the motion tracker will automatically detect the presence of all NPCs within its range (four zones for an M314), even if not within line of sight – but only NPCs who moved in the previous stretch. NPCs who remained stationary in the previous stretch are not detected.

Tracker Pings:

Mark the “pings” of movement from a motion tracker by placing tokens on the map. Such tokens are included in the Hope’s Last Day Starter Set. Detecting an NPC with a motion tracker does not mean that the PC has spotted it – it only means the players get an indication of where an NPC is present.

Example
Stealth Mode

Nat and Hayes are checking out the abnormal engine readings on the USCSS Miranda while carefully looking for the unknown killer on board, moving zone by zone in stealth mode. The GM moves the killer on her hidden GM map. It is currently stationary in the engine control room.

After a few stretches, Nat and Hayes split up as the medic wants to look for a surgical kit. On her turn, Nat moves very carefully into the engine control room, where a horrifying Xenomorph lurks. A passive opposed OBSERVATION roll is made and Nat wins! She takes a chance and decides to hide in the zone, under a control console.

Now, as Nat is hidden and the Xenomorph is not actively looking for her, it does not get to roll OBSERVATION again until Nat moves out of hiding. The GM decides that the Xenomorph moves on its next turn, quickly jumping into an air shaft. Nat is free to come out of hiding to check the engine controls.

Starting A Fight

When a PC spots an NPC or vice versa, stealth mode ends, and initiative is drawn (next page). Draw initiative even if the NPC is not obviously hostile – forcing PCs who act first to decide whether to attack or hold off. Also note that PCs can exchange initiatives with NPCs or each other on their turn (see Holding Off on the next page). Combat can also be initiated by an ambush (next page).

Social Mode

When you stop to interact socially with NPCs, or just stop to relieve stress (page 73) or chat among each other, stealth mode stops. During social mode, the GM estimates how many stretches pass and moves NPCs on the hidden stealth map accordingly. Social mode can also be used when you perform a task that takes more than a stretch to complete. When you start moving around on the map again, stealth mode starts anew.

Example
End of Stealth

Hayes is in the Medbay when the Xenomorph enters the zone via an air duct access point. The GM tells Hayes to roll OBSERVATION but not why, and rolls for the Xeno herself. The opposed roll is a tie, and they spot each other at the same time! Stealth mode ends and combat starts.

Combat & Initiative

WHEN THE ENEMY IS REVEALED, stealth mode is over, and combat starts. Combat is played out in rounds, each approximately 5–10 seconds long. At the start of each round, the first step is to decide who has the initiative – in what order the combatants will act during the round.

Drawing The Initiative

To determine the initiative, ten cards numbered 1 to 10 are used. Such initiative cards are included in the Hope’s Last Day Starter Set, but you can also use ordinary playing cards. Each player in the fight draws a random card at the beginning of each round, and the GM draws cards for NPCs.

The number on the card determines the order in which you act in the round. Card #1 acts first, card #2 acts second, and so forth until everyone has acted. Your place in the initiative order is called your turn. Your “previous turn” or “your next turn” can occur in the current round or the previous/next round, depending on if you’ve had your turn in the current round yet.

Place your initiative card by your character sheet so everyone can see the order in which each person acts. The GM puts their card (or cards) in front of them. When all participants have had their turn, the round is over, and a new round begins by drawing the initiative again.

Xenomorphs have a Speed rating and get one turn – and initiative card – per point of Speed. Read more on page 208.

NPC Initiatives

To make things simple, particularly in large fights, the GM can draw a single initiative card for a group of NPCs. All NPCs in that group act on the same turn in the round. The order among them is decided by the GM. When a PC holds off to swap initiative cards, they swap initiative with the whole NPC group.

Too Many Draws?

Combat in the ALIEN RPG is fast and deadly, and rarely lasts longer than 3–5 rounds. Drawing initiative every round gives a more dynamic flow of play, but should you find it cumbersome, you can draw initiative only in the first round of combat. Holding off and swapping initiative cards can still be done.

Surprise & Ambush

If combat starts with an attack that the GM deems to be surprising, the attacker automatically gets the #1 initiative card in the first round of combat. The other combatants draw their initiative from the cards that remain. With several attackers, they get the lowest (earliest) cards to distribute freely. At the start of the second round, everyone draws the initiative as usual.

Ambush:

A special type of surprise attack is an ambush, which is an attack on an unaware opponent. Ambushes are usually the result of stealth mode (page 56). An ambush also gives the lowest (earliest) initiative card to the attacker(s), and gives +2 dice to the initial attack(s), and the target(s) cannot defend against it or dodge it.

Holding Off

At your turn in the round, you can choose to hold off. This means that you swap initiative cards – and therefore places in the turn order – with another PC or NPC (or NPC group) whose turn comes after yours. They cannot refuse the trade and must take their turn immediately. You cannot swap initiative cards with someone who has already had their turn.

Note: All rules effects that are triggered “on your turn” (such as death rolls, see page 68) are moved in the turn order when initiative cards are swapped. Your turn doesn’t happen until you use it.

Interrupt Actions:

Quick actions that occur out of turn (such as defending or dodging) are not affected by holding off – spent quick actions still count as spent, even if you get a new initiative number. Even if you have spent two quick actions as interrupt actions earlier in the round, and thus don’t get any action on your turn, you can still hold off and swap cards when your turn comes – this can be useful to give another PC an earlier spot in the turn order.

Xenomorphs

(page 208) with a Speed of 2 or more have multiple turns in a single round, and therefore draw multiple initiative cards. When swapping cards with a Xenomorph, you decide which card you want, as long as it comes after your current turn in the initiative order. Xenomorphs themselves rarely hold off, but can if the adventure text or GM says so.

Each round, you can perform one full action and one quick action, or two quick actions.

Full Action:

This is typically an attack but can be anything you could reasonably do in less than 10 seconds that requires your full focus. It typically requires a skill roll. See the adjacent list for examples. A full action can only be performed on your turn in the round.

Quick Action:

These are faster and don’t necessarily demand a skill roll (though some do). Quick actions include movement. See the adjacent list for examples. Some quick actions can be performed out of turn, to interrupt the actions of opponents. On your turn, you can save your quick action for an interrupt action to use later in the round. If you perform two quick actions in the same round, you forfeit your full action.

Action Reset:

All actions are reset at the start of a new combat round. Actions can never be saved from one round to the next.

Giving Orders

As a full action, you can bark orders to another character. They must be able to hear you, even if via a comm radio. Roll for COMMAND. For every you roll, they get +1 die to one roll later in the same round, but only for carrying out the order you gave.

Mark Your Actions

To keep track of your actions in the round, a tip is to keep your initiative face-up and vertical at the start of the round. Turn it horizontally when you perform a quick action. Flip the card over when you perform a full action or your second quick action. When the card is both rotated and flipped, you’re done for the round!

Foot Movement

To move during combat, you can spend a quick action to go from one zone to an adjacent zone, to move up to a door or hatch to peek through it (or take cover by it), or to move between Short and Adjacent range from a character (or item) inside your current zone.

Doors & Hatches:

You can pass an unlocked door or hatch during movement without spending an action. Locking a door or hatch is typically a quick action. A locked door or hatch can be broken down. This requires a close combat attack (full action) with +2 dice. A typical metal door or hatch has armor level 2 and can take 10 points of damage before it gives in. Electronically locked doors or hatches can be opened with a COMTECH roll (full action).

Blocking Movement:

You can, as long as you’re not broken (page 68), block another character from moving past you. As a rule of thumb, you can block a cylindrical volume 2 meters wide and equally tall. To pass you, your opponent must either take you out or make a special close combat action (page 62). Blocking is not mandatory – you can allow friendly characters to pass you.

Crawlspaces:

In a narrow crawlspace such as an air shaft, you cannot move past other characters in the same zone – not even friendlies.

Xenomorphs

often prefer to move through air shafts and other crawlspaces, but can move through rooms and corridors just like human characters, including opening unlocked doors.

Dragging A Person:

You can drag a broken or otherwise immobilized person with you when you move. The person needs to be at Adjacent range, and you need to make a STAMINA roll. If you fail, you remain in the zone.

Leaving Combat

You can attempt to leave a combat before it’s over by moving out of sight of your opponents. If you do, and it’s not obvious where you went (GM’s call), your opponents must roll for OBSERVATION (no action) to understand where you went. If it fails, combat is over, and stealth mode commences again (unless other PCs remain in combat).

Tokens & Miniatures

If you are using official ALIEN RPG tokens or miniatures on a map that is to scale, mark Adjacent range by placing the tokens or miniature bases in contact. If there is no way to move into a zone without placing the tokens or miniatures in contact, the range between them is immediately Adjacent (not Short), without a further move action needed.

You can move into a narrow area on the map (such as a crawlspace) even if your token or miniature base is wider than the space you’re in. However, if the tokens /miniatures of other characters make it impossible to place at least half of your token / base inside a zone (GM’s call), you cannot move into it. Broken characters don’t count.

You can block movement in an area covered by the token or the base of the miniature. Several characters can be placed in base-to-base contact to block wider areas

Hidden Movement In Combat

In combat, movement is usually open for all to see. However, in some situations, the GM may choose to keep NPC movement hidden in combat, using a hidden GM map just like in stealth mode. All other normal movement rules for combat apply.

Example
Holding Off

In the next round, Nat is aware of the Xenomorph and gets to draw initiative. Sadly, she get the #9 card. The Xenomorph draws the #3 and #8, while Hayes — terrified and wounded but still alive — draws the #1 card. Hayes decides to hold off and swaps his card with Nat, allowing her to go first. She spends both her quick and her full action to move, running the two zones over to reach the medbay, just in time to face the Xenomorph.

Close Combat

CLOSE COMBAT is any attack at Adjacent range, no matter what weapon is used (if any). This is a full action. Roll for CLOSE COMBAT to hit. Close combat weapons can give a modifier to your roll (see chapter 5).

Hit:

If the attack succeeds, it inflicts the weapon’s base damage on the target, plus 1 damage point for every rolled beyond the first. Damage may be reduced by body armor (page 67). Unarmed attacks have a base damage of 1.

Weak Spot:

Against an armored target, you can aim for a weak spot, getting –2 dice. On a hit, the armor level is reduced 1 step (after reduction for an armor piercing attack).

Defend

If you are attacked in close combat, you can choose to defend yourself against the attack, to avoid being hit. Defending is a quick action, and you roll for CLOSE COMBAT. You must state that you defend before the attacker rolls for their strike. Defending turns the attack into an opposed roll (page 44) – for each you roll when defending, you eliminate one rolled by the attacker.

Interruption:

Defending is an interrupt action and occurs out of turn. It does require a quick action however – if you have no actions left in the round, you cannot defend. If you fear you will be attacked later in a round, it might be wise to save a quick action for a possible defense.

Special Attacks

In close combat, you may want to achieve something other than just harming your opponent. You must state that you are attempting a special attack before rolling dice, and you cannot use any bonus dice from a weapon. Special attacks can be defended against just like normal attacks. If the attack succeeds, you inflict no damage but instead achieve the desired result:

  • Disarm: You wrestle an item from your opponent’s grasp. You can keep the item or throw it into an adjacent zone as part of the attack.
  • Pass: You move past an enemy blocking your movement (page 61). If successful, place your token or miniature on the other side of the enemy.
  • Shove: You push your opponent to Short range from you. This attack can be used to shove your target through a door or hatch.
  • Grapple: You pin your enemy in a tight clinch. See Grappling below.

Grappling

If you grapple your opponent as a special attack in close combat, they cannot move or perform any actions except trying to break free. This is a full action and an opposed CLOSE COMBAT roll against you (Xenomorphs use MOBILITY instead). While you are grappling, the only action you can perform (until you choose to release your opponent) is a grapple attack. This works as a normal unarmed attack but cannot be defended against.

Close Combat Attacks

Restrained & Unaware Opponents

If your close combat opponent is restrained or unaware of you (see Surprise & Ambush on page 59), they cannot defend themselves against the attack and you get +2 dice to the roll.

Firearms In Close Combat

Note that you can use a firearm even in close combat. You still use the CLOSE COMBAT skill (not RANGED COMBAT) to hit. As most firearms have a minimum range of Short (or more), you typically get –2 dice or more when using them in close combat. Your target can defend against the attack normally.

Example
Close Combat

The Xenomorph attacks Hayes again in close combat, rolling a whopping ten base dices and geting two . Hayes tried to defend himself against the attack. With Strength 3 and no skill level in CLOSE COMBAT, he only rolls three base dices, but with stress level 3 he also get to add three stress dice. He gets a single and luckily, no .

Knowing a single hit from the Xenomorph will likely end him, Hayes decides to push the roll, increasing his stress level to 4 and adding another stress die to his pool. The re-roll produces another , meaning Hayes just barely manage to hold off the ferocious attack.

However, the re-roll also produces a , triggering a stress response. He rolls a D6 getting 6, adding his stress level of 4 and subtracting his Resolve of 5, meaning he's really frantic, getting -2 on all skill rolls based on Strength.

Ranged Combat

WHEN YOU ATTACK SOMEONE at Short distance or more, roll for RANGED COMBAT. You need to have line of sight to your target (page 55). You also need a ranged weapon, even if just something to throw. The weapons tables in chapter 5 describe a variety of firearms and other ranged weapons. Most ranged weapons give a modifier to your roll. Firing a weapon is a full action.

Hit:

If the attack succeeds, it inflicts the weapon’s base damage on the target, plus 1 damage point for every rolled beyond the first (unless the extra are spent to conserve ammo, see below). Damage may be reduced by armor (page 67).

Aiming:

If you take your time to aim carefully before squeez- ing the trigger, you get +2 dice to your roll. Aiming is a quick action and can only be done at Short range or more. The aim must happen on your turn, immediately before the attack. This means you cannot do anything else in the same round.

Range:

Weapons have a minimum and maximum range. Any attack closer than the minimum range gets –2 dice per range band below the minimum range. This does not apply to attacks against unaware or restrained targets. A weapon cannot be used beyond its maximum range – with the exception that you can fire a weapon with Short maximum range into an adjacent zone if you’re standing right next to a door or hatch to that zone.

Weak Spot:

Against an armored target, you can aim for a weak spot, getting –2 dice. On a hit, the armor level is reduced 1 step (after reduction for an armor piercing attack).

Target Size:

Firing at a large target, such as a vehicle, gives +2 dice. Firing at a small object, such as a terminal screen, a hand-held item, or a Chestburster, gives −2 dice.

Dodging

If you are attacked in ranged combat, you can try to dodge the attack. This is a quick action, and you roll for MOBILITY. You can only dodge if you are aware of the attacker, and you must declare a dodge before the attacker rolls. Dodging turns the attack into an opposed roll (page 44) – for each you roll when dodging, you eliminate one rolled by the attacker.

Interruption:

Dodging is an interrupt action and occurs out of turn. It does require a quick action however – if you have no actions left in the round, you cannot dodge. If you fear you will be attacked later in a round, it might be wise to save a quick action for a possible dodge.

Cover

When bullets start flying, you’ll do well to seek cover. It’s also very effective against explosions (page 76). You can only take cover in a cluttered zone, never in an open zone – except by a door or hatch, which gives you cover against enemies in the adjacent zone. There are two types of cover – partial and full.

Partial Cover:

Taking partial cover is a quick action. You ex- pose enough of yourself to be able to see, and shoot at, your enemy. When in partial cover, any ranged attack against you gets –2 dice. You can dodge (above) when in partial cover.

Full Cover:

Completely hiding yourself from view is a full action. Full cover breaks line of sight. Ranged attacks are still possible but get –3 dice and the attacks must penetrate the barrier (page 68). You cannot dodge when in full cover.

Direction Of Cover:

For the sake of simplicity, you don’t need to consider the direction of your cover, it’s assumed to protect you from all sides. The GM may overrule this however, if the situation warrants it.

Friendly Fire

If you fire at a target Adjacent to another character and miss, you risk hitting the unintended target instead. Roll for an attack with two base dice, inflicting base damage if one is rolled and 1 additional damage on two . If there are several Adjacent targets, randomize which one is hit.

The Supply Dail

The supply dial included in the Hope’s Last Day Starter Set can be used to track ammo. Even when using the dial, you still need to track reloads on the character sheet. In Ammo column, there is space to mark both maximum ammo and current ammo for the weapon.

Ammunition

Ranged weapons require ammunition. You don’t need to count individual bullets. Instead, the ammo capacity of each weapon in chapter 5 works as a supply rating (page 29). After each attack, make a supply roll for your weapon. When the ammo supply runs out, you need to reload.

Conserving Ammo:

If you roll more than one for an attack, you can spend one to conserve ammo instead of increasing the damage, meaning no supply roll needs to be made after the attack. You cannot use this rule to fire a weapon with zero ammo left.

Reload:

Reloading a firearm is a quick action, unless stated otherwise. You need to track your available reloads on your character sheet.

Single-Shot:

Weapons designated as single-shot must be reloaded after each shot – no supply roll is made. You cannot use extra to conserve ammo for these.

NPCs:

For NPCs, the GM typically doesn’t make ammo supply rolls. Instead, their ammo capacity is reduced by one step after each attack, unless they spend an extra to conserve ammo. The GM can overrule this at any time it is dramatically appropriate.

Full Auto

Some weapons in chapter 5 can fire bursts of bullets. When you fire a burst and hit without pushing the roll, you can immediately make another attack roll, at the same target or another, as part of the same action. If you hit again (without pushing), you may roll a third time. As soon as you miss, or push a roll, the attack is over. Each attack roll except the first gives you stress level +1. You cannot make more than three attack rolls in a single action.

Ammo:

When firing a burst, you must make an ammo supply roll after each attack roll. You cannot use extra rolled in the attack roll to conserve ammo.

Aiming:

If you aim carefully (page 64), the bonus will only apply to the first attack roll.

Example
Ranged Combat

Nat, at Short range from the Xenomorph, raises her Rexim RXF-M5 EVA Pistol at the Xenomorph, using her quick action to aim carefully and her full action to fire. With no skill level in RANGED COMBAT, she needs all the bonuses she can get. She rolls six base dice (three for her Agility 3, +1 for her Rexim, and +2 for aiming) and two stress dice (her current stress level is 2), getting two , Nat could use the extra to conserve ammo but decides to maximize the damage. The Rexim has a base damage rating of 1, meaning the shot hits for 2 points of damage.

Damage

IN COMBAT, you run the risk of being injured. Everything from exhaustion to bleeding cuts and broken bones is summarized as damage. How much damage you can take before being broken is determined by your Health score. Your starting Health score is equal to the average of your Strength and Agility scores, rounded up. The Hardened talent can increase your maximum Health.

Armor

To protect yourself from harm, you can wear body armor (chapter 5). Xenomorphs often have natural body armor, and vehicles can also provide protection. The effectiveness of armor is represented by its armor level. You can only wear one suit of armor at a time.

When you take damage from an external attack, the damage caused is reduced by the armor level.

Armor Piercing:

Against attacks designated as armor piercing, the armor level counts as 1 step lower than its normal value (to a minimum of zero).

Weak Spots:

Attacks in both close and ranged combat can be aimed at weak spots in the armor. This gives the attack a –2 modifier, but on a hit, the armor level is reduced 1 step (in addition to any reduction for an armor piercing attack).

No Weapons Of Any Kind

You want to be careful where you point that thing in space. Firefights onboard spacecraft, space stations, and within pressure domes can be disastrous – a stray bullet can rupture the hull and vent an entire living sector into space (page 75). People have been blown out into space through the tiniest of tears in the hull – a gruesome way to go, for sure.

Because of this, weapons are limited on spacecraft. Most military smart guns won’t fire when turned on a target along the outer hull of a safe-sensor equipped ship. Civilian weapons aren’t quite so clever. Your best bet is to keep ‘em holstered until you’ve exhausted all other options. Then hold on to something tight and make for an airlock.

Barriers

Barriers such as spaceship bulkheads also have armor levels – see the adjacent table for examples. When a ranged attack hits a barrier, the damage is reduced by the armor level. If the damage is reduced to zero, the attack does not breach the barrier.

Breach Limit:

For barriers (and vehicles, see page 79), if the armor level is higher than the base damage rating of the weapon (not just equal), after any modifiers for ammunition and weak spots, the attack cannot breach the armor, no matter how many are rolled.

Cover:

Note that partial cover (page 66) does not provide an armor level – only full cover will do that. If you are in full cover behind a barrier and wear body armor, first apply the effect of the cover, then the armor.

Collateral Damage:

In some zones, such as next to an outer bulkhead of a spaceship or a nuclear reactor, a stray bullet can trigger catastrophic effects. In such locations, the GM rolls one stress die each time a ranged attack that could potentially breach the relevant barrier (i.e. base damage ≥ modified armor level) misses its target. On a , the barrier is breached.

Broken

If you drop to zero Health, you are broken – in effect, taken out of the action. Immediately roll for a critical injury (page 70). If you’re not dead, you can make a single move action per round and mumble through the pain, but not perform any other actions. You can’t go below zero Health, but any further damage gives you another critical injury. While broken, you cannot gain more stress and never make panic rolls. There are three ways to get back up after being broken, assuming you’re not dead:

  • First Aid: Someone at Adjacent range can administer first aid with a AID roll (full action, modified for medical gear). On a success, you are no longer broken and recover 1 point of Health per rolled. If you suffer from a lethal critical injury, you are also stabilized (below).
  • Rally: Another character in the same zone can try to rally you with a COMMAND roll (quick action). On a success, you are no longer broken and recover 1 point of Health per rolled. Rallying has no effect on critical injuries.
  • On Your Own: If you’re broken (but not dead) and no one gives you first aid, you automatically get back up after one stretch of time and recover 1 point of Health. This has no effect on critical injuries.

Recovery:

As long as you are not fatigued (page 75), you automatically recover 1 point of Health per stretch of time spent resting in a safe area (even if suffering from a critical injury). First aid and rallying can only recover Health when you are broken.

Critical Injuries

As long as you still have Health left, damage points represent fatigue, bruises or smaller cuts – painful, to be sure, but possible to overcome. Critical injuries represent a much more dangerous form of injury. These can maim or kill you. When knocked down to zero Health, roll a D66 on the table on page 70. If you roll a critical injury you already have (result #14 and above), re-roll.

Death:

If you suffer a critical injury listed as lethal, you must make a death roll after the listed time limit has passed. If the time limit is round, roll on your every turn, right after you act. A death roll is a roll for STAMINA (not an action), but you cannot push it, and you are not allowed to roll any stress dice. If you fail a death roll, you die. If you succeed, you linger on, but you must make another death roll when the time limit has passed again.

Broken NPC

NPCs can be broken in the same ways as PCs. An NPC can rally or give first aid to a PC and vice versa. However, dice are usually not rolled when an NPC aids another NPC – instead, the GM decides what happens. The GM can also decide that a minor NPC who is broken simply dies.

Handling Death

The death of a PC can be traumatic but try not to see it as a failure. Instead, consider it a dramatic peak in your story and then get back into the action as soon as possible. In cinematic adventures, character deaths are quite common – and can be very memorable events in the story.

Example
Character Death

The Xenomorph, ignoring Nat, strikes Hayes again. This time, he fails to defend himself and goes to zero Health, broken. He rolls for a critical injury and gets 62 — a massive hemorrhage!

Nat tries to give Hayes first aid to stop the bleeding with a MEDICAL AID roll but fails. Desperate, she then uses her quick action for a COMMAND roll to rally Hayes. This succeeds, and Hayes joins the fight again.

On his next turn, Hayes fights valiantly against the beast, shouting at Nat to get out to save herself. Then make a death roll. Sadly, the STAMINA roll fails, and Nat can only watch in horror as Hayes collapses on the floor, dead.

First Aid:

To save your life when you have suffered a lethal critical injury, someone must give you first aid to stabilize you before you fail a death roll. This is a full action and requires a MEDICAL AID roll, modified for medical gear. If you are no longer broken, you can try to administer first aid on yourself, but with −2 dice.

  • If the roll succeeds, you are stabilized and can stop making death rolls. If you were broken, you also recover 1 point of Health per rolled. The other effects of your critical injury remain until healed.
  • If the roll fails, your condition worsens – the time limit is decreased one step, for example from shift to stretch, or stretch to round. If the time limit was already round, you perish.

On Your Own:

If you succeed at three death rolls on your own, you survive without help and can stop rolling. Instant Kill: Note that there are three critical injuries (results #64–66) that kill you outright. If you roll any of these, you’re dead. No death rolls, no chance of surviving – you’re done for. Some Xenomorph signature attacks (page 208) can also be instantly fatal. Finally, if the damage from a single instance equals or exceeds double your maximum Health, you are killed outright.

Healing:

Each critical injury has a specific effect that you suffer during the healing time indicated, which is measured in days unless stated otherwise. Several critical injuries can heal simultaneously. Each day during which someone with the Nurse talent tends to you while healing counts double. Note that you can recover all your lost Health points but still suffer the effects of a critical injury.

Surgery:

Some critical injuries require surgery before they start to heal – this takes a shift of time and requires a MEDICAL AID roll (with bonuses for talents and medical gear).

Stun Damage

Some weapons inflict stun damage instead of normal damage. Attacks from such weapons don’t reduce your Health – instead, when hit you need to make a STAMINA roll (no action) with a negative modifier equal to the damage inflicted. If you fail, you can’t perform any action on your next turn, nor any quick actions until then. On your next turn, roll STAMINA again, this time unmod- ified. If you fail again, you’re stunned until your next turn after that, and so on until you make a successful STAMINA roll. The STAMINA rolls can be pushed normally, and armor works normally against stun damage.

Panic Rolls

BEING IN THE COLD DARKNESS of space will test your nerves even under normal circumstances. Add horrifying Xenomorphs, and you really need to keep your nerves in check to avoid spiraling into a wild panic.

Stress Level

The mounting tension in your character is measured by your stress level. It usually starts at zero and increases during the course of the game. Your stress can increase in many ways, such as:

  • You push a skill roll (page 43).
  • You or a nearby PC suffers a certain stress or panic response (next page).
  • A nearby NPC panics (as determined by the GM).
  • You fire a second or third burst of full auto fire (page 67).
  • You become fatigued (page 75).
  • A member of your own crew attacks you.
  • A person nearby is revealed to be an android.
  • Any unnerving encounter as determined by the adventure or the GM.
Panic Roll
1 Roll D6.
2 Add your stress level.
3 Subtract your Resolve.
4 Check the panic response table.

Panic Roll

As long as you keep your stress in check, you can use it to your advantage. But if the tension grows too strong it can explode, sending you into a wild panic. You need to make a panic roll when any of the following happens:

  • You witness another PC become broken.
  • You see a horrifying Xenomorph for the first time.
  • A terrifying Xenomorph comes within Adjacent range (even if you have seen it before).
  • You witness another PC suffer from a certain panic response.
  • A truly horrifying event occurs, as determined by the adventure or the GM.

Resolution:

Roll a D6, add your current stress level, subtract your Resolve rating, and compare the result to the table on the next page.

More Panic:

If you are suffering from a panic response and are forced to make another panic roll, you suffer both responses if possible. In cases where two responses cannot be combined, the higher response takes precedence. If you already have a panic response (or stress response of the same name) and roll the same again, use the next higher response on the table.

Stopping Panic

Some panic responses are immediate or last until your next turn. If this is not the case, the effect remains until one of the following happens:

  • Another character within speaking range (including comms) makes a COMMAND roll. This is a full action in combat.
  • You are broken.
  • One stretch passes.

Relieving Stress

For every stretch of time (5–10 minutes) spent resting in a safe area, your stress level is reduced by 1 point. In addition, one stress response (page 44) of your choice is removed for each stress level reduced. You can’t make skill rolls when resting, and if your rest is interrupted, it doesn’t count. A panic roll can also decrease stress, as can certain drugs (page 98).

Other Hazards

THE WORLD OF ALIEN is unforgiving, and there are many ways for your character to suffer and die.

Fatigue

If you don’t get enough air, water, food, or sleep, or if you are exposed to extreme cold or heat without adequate protection, you must make SURVIVAL rolls at regular intervals. As soon as you fail such a roll, you become fatigued. Mark this on your character sheet. Being fatigued has several effects:

  • You can no longer recover Health.
  • You must continue to make SURVIVAL rolls at the same intervals. For each failed roll, you suffer 1 point of damage and gain +1 stress level.
  • If broken by damage from fatigue, don’t roll for a critical injury. Instead, make death rolls at the same interval as the SURVIVAL rolls. First aid has no effect against these rolls – only removing the source of the fatigue will stop the death rolls.

Suffocation:

If your supply of air runs out (page 28), or if under water, you must make a SURVIVAL roll every round (in combat, the roll does not count as an action).

Hunger & Thirst:

As a rule of thumb, you need to eat and drink something every day. After a full 24 hours without food and water, you must make SURVIVAL rolls once per shift.

Sleep Deprivation:

You must sleep at least one shift per day. After a full 24 hours without sleep, you must make SURVIVAL rolls once per shift. If you are broken by a SURVIVAL roll caused by sleep deprivation, don’t make death rolls – instead, you pass out and sleep for a full shift, impossible to wake. Certain drugs (page 98) can help against sleep deprivation.

Heat & Cold:

How often you need to make SURVIVAL rolls due to extreme temperatures depends on the situation, and is decided by the adventure text or the GM. It can be once per day, shift, stretch, or even round (in combat, the roll does not count as an action).

Hypersleep:

Spending any length of time in hypersleep will make you fatigued. Hypersleep fatigue is remedied after one shift awake, assuming you eat a sturdy meal. The effect can also be counteracted by the Hydr8tion drug (page 98).

Recovery:

As soon as the hazardous situation is remedied, you are no longer fatigued and stop making SURVIVAL rolls. If broken, you can then be stabilized. Androids are completely immune to all forms of fatigue.

Vacuum

Space is a cold and unforgiving place. If your ship suffers explosive decompression (below) or if you are thrown out of an airlock, your life is in extreme danger. The absence of pressure creates gas bubbles in your blood, causing your whole body to swell up. This results in crippling pain, all while the raw UV radiation from the nearest star sears your skin.

In a vacuum, you suffer +1 stress level each round and you must make a SURVIVAL roll before you can take action. The roll itself is not an action. The first roll is unmodified, but you get −1 dice on the second roll, –2 on the third roll, and so on. A failed roll means you drop directly to zero Health and become broken. Don’t roll for a critical injury – instead, make a death roll every round. Nothing can stop these death rolls except escaping the vacuum.

Climbing into a space suit quickly (full action) requires a successful MOBILITY roll.

Explosive Decompression

Firing guns inside a spacecraft is very dangerous. If a shot misses its target in a zone next to an outer bulkhead that can potentially be breached by the shot (page 79), the GM rolls one stress die. On a , the barrier is breached. If a character intentionally tries to shoot a hole in the hull, make a normal ranged attack with +2 dice.

A hull breach is bad news for everyone. The air will vent out into space at a rate of one stretch per affected zone, and the intense draft will require everyone to make a SURVIVAL roll on their turn to perform any action (this roll itself is not an action).

Most ships will automatically seal off a breached zone. Once the air is gone, anyone still in the vented zone will suffer the effects of vacuum.

Falling

Falling on a hard surface in gravity automatically inflicts an amount of damage to you equal to the height of the fall (in meters) divided by 2, rounding all fractions down. In a controlled jump, roll MOBILITY – each rolled reduces the damage done by 1. Body armor does not protect you.

Zero Gravity

Most ships and stations have artificial gravity generators, but these can fail. You can also find yourself spacewalking outside your ship. In a zone without gravity, all rolls for CLOSE COMBAT, HEAVY MACHINERY, MOBILITY and RANGED COMBAT get –2 dice. In addition, every move requires a successful MOBILITY roll (without the penalty), with failure meaning you spin in place and suffer +1 stress level. The Zero-G Training talent removes all of the above effects.

On the flip side, zero gravity allows you to move in any direction, floating through open air (or space). And you never risk falling damage.

Explosions

Some weapons in chapter 5 have an explosive effect. You fire these as a normal ranged attack, but if you hit your target, all other characters in the same zone (PC and NPCs alike) suffer the same damage. Each target can dodge the attack separately. If your attack misses, the shot is assumed to have gone astray and hurts no one.

Cover:

For the primary target, cover page 66) has the same effect as in any ranged attack. Secondary targets in partial cover count as if they were in full cover (i.e. they get protection from the full armor level of the barrier).

Contained spaces:

If you fire an explosive weapon into a zone with only blocked borders (via a hatch or door from an adjacent zone, or inside the zone behind cover), you get +2 dice to the attack.

Placed Explosives:

Detonations caused by anything that’s not a ranged attack have a blast power and a base damage. The base damage is equal to one third of the blast power (round up to the nearest whole number). When the explosion occurs, roll for an attack with a number of base dice equal to the blast power. If the attack hits, every PC and NPC in the zone suffers the base damage plus 1 point of damage for each rolled beyond the first. This is not a skill roll and cannot be pushed. All targets behind partial or full cover get protection from the full armor level of the barrier).

Fire

A fire typically covers one zone and is measured by its intensity. A typical fire has intensity 6–9. When entering a burning zone, or starting a round in one, roll a number of base dice equal to the intensity. For every rolled, you suffer 1 point of damage. Body armor has its normal effect. If you are broken by fire damage, don’t roll for a critical injury – instead, make a death roll every round until you’re out of the fire.

Catching Fire:

If you take damage from a fire attack, you catch fire and continue to burn, suffering another fire attack at the start of each new round even if you leave the burning zone. As soon as a fire attack outside the burning zone inflicts no damage, you’re no longer on fire. You, or a friend at Adjacent range, can stop you from burning with a successful MOBILITY roll (full action).

Fire Spreading:

For a typical fire, roll a D6 at the start of each round. On 1–2, the fire goes out and the zone is no longer considered burning. On a 5–6, the fire spreads to a random adjacent zone, assuming the zone border is open and there is something in the adjacent zone that can burn. Specialized gear can be used to extinguish fires.

Disease

When exposed to a dangerous contagion or infection, you need to roll for STAMINA to avoid falling sick. This is a passive roll, and cannot be pushed. The GM may even roll for you in secret.

Virulence:

Contagions have a virulence rating, which is a negative modifier to your STAMINA roll. If the virulence is not listed, it is zero.

Effects:

Diseases can have a wide range of effects. A common effect is to make you fatigued (page 75), triggering further STAMINA rolls (instead of SURVIVAL) modified for virulence at regular intervals (typically once per shift), with the normal effects of fatigue if you fail. Once you succeed, your immune system has beaten the disease, and you can start to recover.

Medical Aid:

If someone cares for you while you are sick, they can roll for MEDICAL AID to fight off the disease, replacing your STAMINA rolls. Medical gear can give bonuses to this.

Radiation

The world of ALIEN contains many places where you will be exposed to hard radiation – on a spacewalk near a dying star, for example, or when you try to repair your ship’s leaking reactor core.

Radiation Level:

When you are exposed to radiation, you gain radiation points, or rads, that accumulate in your body. Mark your rads on your character sheet. The area’s radiation level determines how often you get rads.

  • WEAK RADIATION: 1 rad per shift
  • STRONG RADIATION: 1 rad per stretch
  • EXTREME RADIATION: 1 rad per round

Effects:

Every time you gain a radiation point, you must roll a number of dice equal to your total current number of accumulated rads. For every rolled, you suffer 1 point of damage. If you are broken by radiation damage, don’t roll for a critical injury – instead, make death rolls at the same intervals as above until you’re out of the hotspot.

Recovery:

As long as you remain in a hotspot, you cannot recover Health in any way. After you leave the irradiated area, you heal one rad per shift.

Synthetics

Synthetic individuals are increasingly common in the Core Systems, and appear on the Frontier as well. Some are open about their artificial nature, others pose as humans. In combat, androids act like humans, and they make skill rolls normally. But there are several differences:

Attributes:

Synthetics generally have higher attribute score than humans.

Skills & Stress:

Androids can’t push skill rolls. They don’t suffer stress, don’t have a stress level, and they never make panic rolls.

Damage:

If a synthetic goes to zero Health, don’t roll for a normal critical injury. Instead, roll for critical systems damage on the table above. Until the android suffers a system shutdown (result #6), it can continue to operate, only suffering the effects of the critical damage. This makes androids very hard to stop.

If an android at zero Health suffers further damage, roll again on the critical system damage. If you roll a damage that the android already has, shift it up one step on the table (from a #4 to a #5, for example).

Synthetics don’t need air, food, water, or sleep, and thus never suffer the effects of fatigue (page 75). They are immune to vacuum, cold, and disease. Androids are typically unaffected by alien pathogens.

Androids don’t heal. Instead, a shift of work and a COMTECH roll is required to repair all lost Health points and all critical system damage, as long as the android hasn’t suffered a system shutdown. They can repair themselves.

An android that suffers a system shutdown is damaged beyond repair, but can be reactivated only to communicate with. This requires a power source, a stretch of work, and a COMTECH roll. Make a power supply roll after each stretch of communication.

Electric Sheep

It is illegal to program an android for violence against humans. While modern synthetics are governed by behavioral inhibitors that prevent them from injuring others, earlier models are not so equipped and are therefore dangerous. Expensive bypass packages are available on the black market for those who want to circumvent these laws.

Xenomorphs

You’ve heard the stories about the monsters out there. You better hope that you won’t need to face one yourself. Fighting Xenomorph beings is something different entirely and far more deadly than facing humans or even androids. Very few have lived to tell the tale.

A range of Xenomorphs are described in detail in chapter 10. In combat, Xenomorphs follow special rules. These are described on page 208 in the GM’s section.

Vehicle Combat

A WIDE RANGE OF VEHICLES, both of the air and ground varieties, are used to get around planetside on the Frontier. You’ll find a selection described in chapter 5.

Vehicle Attributes

  • MANEUVERABILITY: A modifier to your PILOTING rolls when performing challenging maneuvers.
  • SPEED: The speed of the vehicle, in number of zones per driving action.
  • HULL: How much damage the vehicle can take before becoming wrecked.
  • ARMOR: The armor level of the vehicle.

Vehicles In Combat

Driving under normal circumstances requires no dice rolls, but more advanced maneuvers require PILOTING rolls. Entering or mounting a vehicle is a quick action. Starting a motorized vehicle is also a quick action.

Movement:

Driving a vehicle is a quick action, and it will move you a number of zones up to the Speed rating of your ride. Note that you can drive twice in the same round if you spend both of your actions on it.

Vehicle Armaments:

Some vehicles are fitted with mounted heavy weapons, fired by the crew (and in some cases, the driver). You’ll find a list of vehicle weapons on page 313.

Ramming:

Most vehicles can be used as weapons – i.e. to simply ram your enemy. It counts as a close combat attack, but roll for PILOTING instead of CLOSE COMBAT. Add the vehicle’s Maneuverability rating. The base damage rating of the attack equals your vehicle’s Hull rating divided by 2, rounding up.

Damage To Vehicles

When a vehicle has suffered damage equal to or in excess of its Hull rating, it is wrecked. This means that the vehicle becomes inoperable and everyone inside takes 3 points of damage, mitigated by a MOBILITY roll (no action) – each reduces the damage by one point.

Armor Level:

Most vehicles have an armor level, which reduces the amount of incoming damage.

Breach Limit:

For vehicles (and barriers, see page 68), if the armor level is higher than the base damage rating of the weapon (not just equal), after any modifiers for ammunition and weak spots, the attack cannot breach the armor, no matter how many are rolled.

Component Damage:

If a vehicle takes damage equal to or more than half its Hull rating (rounding up) in a single hit, it suffers component damage. Roll a D6 and consult the adjacent table.

Exposed Passengers:

Passengers in an open vehicle, or sitting on top of a vehicle, can be targeted separately. A partially exposed character is considered to be in partial cover (page 66) but not shielded by the armor level of the vehicle.

Ramming Vehicles:

You can use your vehicle to ram another vehicle. Roll the attack like you would if you were ramming a person (above), but both vehicles suffer the base damage. Extra damage due to additional is inflicted on the target only.

Aerial Vehicles

Aerial vehicles are handled in much the same way as ground vehicles, with some exceptions.

Altitude:

At all times you must keep track of the vehicle’s altitude, measured in zones. When moving, you distribute the zones moved on vertical and horizontal movement. On the ground, the Speed of an aerial vehicle is typically 1.

Crashes:

If an aerial vehicle is wrecked, it crashes violently to the ground. All people inside suffer damage equal to the altitude (in zones) multiplied by 2. You can brace yourself for the hit by rolling MOBILITY (not an action) – each rolled reduces the damage by 1.

Repairs

Repairing vehicle damage requires one or several HEAVY MACHINERY rolls. One roll can be made per shift. Only one person can roll to repair the hull, but others can help. Each removes 1 point of damage. If the vehicle was wrecked, it is no longer considered wrecked once it has regained at least 1 point of Hull.

Component Damage:

If the engine or a mounted weapon has been disabled, repairing it typically requires a HEAVY MACHINERY roll, in addition to any roll for repairing Hull damage. The work takes one shift.
